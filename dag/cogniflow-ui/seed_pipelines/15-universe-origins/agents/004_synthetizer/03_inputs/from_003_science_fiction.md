# The Loom of the Unlit Hour

Before there was a Before, there dreamed a silence so complete it had weight. It was not darkness, for darkness is the absence of something, and there was nothing yet for light to leave. It was a pale, breathless hush that stretched in no direction, a hush in which the idea of distance had not been invented. Through this hush drifted the Unlit Weaver, who was neither woman nor man nor beast, but a long slow thought that had not yet learned it was thinking. Around her wrists, had she possessed wrists, coiled threads of a substance older than substance — strands spun from maybes, from almosts, from the trembling edges of a yawn.

For an eternity that was not yet eternity, the Weaver held her threads and did not weave. She was waiting, though she did not know for what. Then, at the hollow heart of her, something small and bright opened its eyes. Some myths call it Curiosity; some call it the First Ache. Whatever its name, it leaned out of her chest and asked the only question the hush had ever heard: *What if?*

The question struck the threads like a struck bell. They began to hum. They began to burn. The Weaver, startled into being, threw her hands wide, and the strands flew from her fingers in long shuddering arcs — warp flung above, weft flung below, each fibre tuning itself as it went. Where two threads crossed, a star was knotted. Where a thread doubled back on itself, a galaxy coiled like a sleeping snail. Where threads frayed, they became rivers; where they snapped, they became the hollow shapes of valleys waiting for rain. The Weaver wept, not from sorrow but from the unbearable newness of having edges, and her tears cooled into the first oceans, which is why even now the sea tastes of astonishment.

When at last the loom slackened and the shuttle slowed, a hesitant glimmer pushed through the lattice: the shy, uncertain dawn of the first morning, testing its colours against the rim of the new world. It found pale grasses that had only just agreed to be green. It found mountains still deciding their heights. It found a small creature, no larger than a heartbeat, lifting its face to be warmed — and in that lifting, the Universe recognised itself for the first time, and, recognising, agreed at last to stay.

And the Weaver, emptied of thread, folded into the spaces between things, where she waits still, listening for the next *What if?*
